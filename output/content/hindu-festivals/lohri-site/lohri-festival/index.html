Lohri Festival – Meaning, Significance & Celebrations in India

Lohri is a popular winter harvest festival celebrated primarily in Punjab, Haryana, Himachal Pradesh, and parts of North India. The festival marks the end of winter and the harvesting of rabi crops, especially sugarcane. Lohri is celebrated with bonfires, traditional songs, dancing, and sharing of festive food.

What Is Lohri Festival?

Lohri is a seasonal folk festival associated with the harvest of crops and seasonal change. People gather around a bonfire, offer traditional items like sesame seeds, jaggery, peanuts, and popcorn, and pray for prosperity and happiness.

Why Is Lohri Celebrated?

  • To mark the end of peak winter
  • To celebrate the harvest of sugarcane and rabi crops
  • To welcome longer days after the winter solstice
  • To express gratitude to Surya (Sun) and Agni (Fire)

In Punjabi culture, Lohri also holds special importance for newly married couples and families celebrating the birth of a child.

History of Lohri Festival

Lohri is rooted in Punjabi folklore and agrarian traditions. A popular legend links Lohri to Dulla Bhatti, a local hero celebrated in folk songs for helping the poor and protecting the vulnerable.

When Is Lohri Celebrated?

Lohri is typically celebrated on 13 January. For the exact annual date and day, visit Lohri Date.

How Is Lohri Celebrated?

  • Lighting a bonfire (Lohri Agni)
  • Singing Punjabi folk songs
  • Performing Bhangra and Gidda
  • Circling the bonfire and offering prasad
  • Sharing sweets and seasonal snacks

Lohri Bonfire Ritual

The bonfire is the heart of Lohri celebrations. Offerings like til, gur, peanuts, popcorn, rewri, and gajak are made to the fire. Learn more at Bonfire Significance.
